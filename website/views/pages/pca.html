Principal Component Analysis (PCA)

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a widely used statistical technique for reducing the dimensionality of data while retaining as much of its variability as possible. It works by transforming the data into a new coordinate system where the greatest variances lie on the first axes (called principal components).

Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

At the core of PCA are eigenvalues and eigenvectors. Eigenvectors determine the directions of the new feature space (the principal components), while eigenvalues indicate the amount of variance captured by each component. A larger eigenvalue means that component explains more of the data's variability. By ranking components according to eigenvalues, we decide which ones to keep.

Dimensionality Reduction

Dimensionality reduction is the process of reducing the number of features in a dataset while preserving its essential patterns. High-dimensional datasets are often difficult to analyze and visualize because of redundancy and noise. PCA addresses this by projecting data onto fewer dimensions that capture the most variance, simplifying both analysis and visualization.

Why is Dimensionality Reduction Important?

High-dimensional data (sometimes called the "curse of dimensionality") poses challenges such as increased computational cost, difficulty in visualization, and risk of overfitting. PCA helps to address these challenges by keeping only the most informative features, reducing noise, and making patterns in the data clearer. This makes clustering, classification, and visualization more effective.
